Indra’s Net

It was only in the late 1980s that the technology to present the results of such work in an appropriately designed format became widely enough available to qualify as, at least, a potential medium of publication.15 It was at this time that I produced the first published piece in a new medium of my own making, Indra’s Net, a title which I used for this piece and also for the series of works which have followed from it.16

Indra’s Net was one of two metaphors which guided the inception and development of this cybertextual project. The concept of Indra’s Net originates in Hinduism. The net was made of jewels and hung in the palace of the god Indra, a generative representation of the structure of the universe. I first encountered it in a history of Chinese Buddhism: “a network of jewels that not only reflect the images in every other jewel, but also the multiple images in the others.”17 As a metaphor of universal structure, it was used by the Chinese Huayan Buddhists to exemplify the “interpenetration and mutual identification” of underlying substance and specific forms. In my own work it refers to the identification of underlying linguistic structures which are used to restructure given texts recursively, and so to postulate and demonstrate these structures’ generative literary potential; or, on a more grandiose scale, to represent some of the underlying principles of meaning-creation within language itself, those which generate new language in the same way that the universe may be seen to be formed by the falling and ‘swerving’ atoms of Lucretius.18

The other metaphor which helps to structure my work is taken from holography. The neologism, ‘hologography’, is based on the definition of ‘hologram’ in the Shorter Oxford English Dictionary: “A pattern produced when light (or other radiation) reflected, diffracted, or transmitted by an object placed in a coherent beam (e.g. from a laser) is allowed to interfere with the undiffracted beam; a photographic plate or film containing such a pattern.” This is transposed from light into language: “A pattern of language produced when the words or the orders of words in a given text are glossed, paraphrased, etymologized, acrostically or otherwise transformed, and such transformations are allowed to interfere with the given text; a set of rules, a machine or a computer program which defines or displays such a pattern.”

INDRA’S NET

Term taken from Hinduism, but uncovered when reading about its use in illustrating certain doctrines of the Chinese Huayan school of Buddhism. INDRA’S NET was ‘a network of jewels that not only reflected the images in every other jewel, but also the multiple images in the others.’ (Ch’en, Buddhism in China, Princeton, 1964) It was a metaphor of universal structure and was used in Huayan Buddhism to exemplify the ‘interpenetration and mutual identification’ of underlying substance and specific forms.

INDRA’S NET

Used by analogy to describe texts where the letters and words, or words and their glosses or etymologies ‘inter-penetrate and are mutually identified’. The structure of these texts allows the same sort of iterative, infinitely regressive form implied by the jewels of Indra’s Net and the reflections which they manifest.

POEMPOEMS

Oskar Pastior (1927– ), in describing the processes he used to compose his Poempoems compared them to those of: ‘holography … to make a text as far as possible such that every part contains the whole. That is an image I hold in front of me.’ (Pastior, ‘Poempoems’, The Printed Head, Vol. I, no. 5, London: Atlas Press, 1991)

HOLOGRAM

(as defined by the Shorter Oxford English Dictionary ) A pattern produced when light (or other radiation) reflected, diffracted, or transmitted by an object placed in a coherent beam (e.g. from a laser) is allowed to interfere with the undiffracted beam; a photographic plate or film containing such a pattern.

HOLOGOGRAM

(From Holo- and Logogram. Popularly: Indra’s Net) A pattern of language produced when the words of a base text are glossed, paraphrased, etymologized, acrostically or otherwise transformed, and such transformations are allowed to interfere with the words of the base text; a set of rules, a machine or a computer program which defines or displays such a pattern.

INDRA’S POEMPOEM: THREE DIMENSIONS IN LITERATURE

The visual hologram represents three dimensions in two, and the literary hologogram performs a similar feat. Letters are the graphic atoms of literary representation. Imagine the words of a text set, as usual, in two dimensions on a plane (a piece of transparent paper). Tilt the plane slightly and suspend it in space. Now imagine invisible lines forming an irregular grid in the third dimension and emanating from each of the letters of the text both above and below the words lying on the plane. Along each of these lines—in what will be an ‘acrostic’ dimension, arrange all the other words of the text which share the letter from which a particular line emanates.

What you now see is an irregular solid, a constellation of words in three dimensional space associated and structured by two very different criteria:— through their membership of the set of words which composes a particular text, and because they share a particular literary/graphic element.

A ‘Free Abecedarian or Internal Acrostic Indra’s Net’ is simply a set of readings along the lines of the acrostic dimension of this virtual, three dimensional constellation of related words.

Again for drafting purposes: Descriptions of the pieces from the HyperCard stack.

UNDER IT ALL

UNDER IT ALL is produced in two versions from the same base text, a short piece of prose by John Cayley.

UNDER IT ALL II, which is perhaps more interesting to see ‘read’, is a ‘free abecedarian internal-acrostic indra’s net poem’. UNDER IT ALL is a ‘strict abecedarian or head-acrostic’ version.

OVERHEARD IN A PUB

OVERHEARD IN A PUB is the only example in this book of a TWENTY-SIX-WORD-STORY ABECEDARIAN OR HEAD-ACROSTIC INDRA’S NET POEM.

LIVING WORDS

LIVING WORDS is an example of a ‘composed’, non-hologographic poem, where the computer is not strictly neccessary (only, perhaps, desirable as a ‘reading’ device).

The entire reading takes about 5 minutes.

LIVING WORDS is a NON-HOLOGOGRAPHIC ETYMO-GLOSSOLOGICAL INDRA’S NET POEM.

HIEROGLYPHICS

HIEROGLYPHICS is a HOLOGOGRAPHIC ETYMO- GLOSSOLOGICAL INDRA’S NET POEM, where the verses defining certain of the key words in the short text are composed of words taken exclusively from the text itself. (LIVING WORDS is a non-hologographic example of this type of Indra’s Net.)

A REFINEMENT OF LANGUAGE

A REFINEMENT OF LANGUAGE is a ‘free abecedarian internal-acrostic indra’s net poem’ based on a short quotation from one of Ezra Pound’s CANTOS.

DUNCAN’S FIRE II

DUNCAN’S FIRE II is a ‘free abecedarian internal-acrostic indra’s net poem’ based on the opening of a poem by Robert Duncan.

CRITICAL THEORY

CRITICAL THEORY is a ‘free abecedarian internal-acrostic indra’s net poem’. The text, by John Cayley, was commissioned by the Zürich-based artist, Christopher Bledowski. It is a series of nine loosely structured statements — often based on the artist’s own words or quotations he has used — the whole intended as an appreciation of Bledowski’s production.

INDRA’S POEMPOEM

INDRA’S POEMPOEM is a ‘free abecedarian internal- acrostic indra’s net poem’ based on a short explantory piece concerning the three dimensional qualities of this type of work which appears amongst the introductory pages of this book.
